FAQ

What do the buttons at the top do?

Scale picks the root note and scale/arpeggio type. Tempo sets the BPM (or tap it in). Audio Cues controls what gets spoken/played about the notes themselves — piano sound, note names, finger numbers, cross-hand cues, voice cues. Metronome is the click track underneath everything — sound, subdivision, groove style, accents, swing. Practice controls which hand plays and how (see below). Settings is theme and screen size.

What's the difference between Audio Cues and Metronome?

Audio Cues are about the notes — hearing the piano, hearing "3, C" spoken for a finger + note name. Metronome is about the beat — the click track, its rhythm feel, and its accent pattern. They're independent: you can have a metronome running with the piano and voice muted, or vice versa.

What do Full / Up / Down mean in Practice?

Full plays the scale ascending then descending. Up plays only the ascent, Down only the descent. Pick which hand(s) play with Right Hand / Left Hand / Both Hands above those buttons.

What are the Cross Over / Cross Under arcs?

Scales that span more than 5 notes in one hand need a finger to cross under the thumb (or the thumb to cross under a finger) to keep going. The curved arc on the piano roll and sheet music, plus the spoken cue, mark exactly where that crossing happens.

What do Pre-count and Speed Ramp do?

Pre-count counts off a few beats before the scale actually starts, like a metronome count-in. Speed Ramp automatically nudges the tempo up by a set amount every few loops, so you can practice gradually building speed without manually adjusting the tempo yourself.

Why does the keyboard show more keys than the scale uses?

The piano roll always shows an extra octave of context on either side of whatever notes the current scale plays, so it reads like a real keyboard instead of a cropped sliver sized exactly to the notes.

What does the Size setting do, and why did it pick one for me?

Mobile / Tablet / Computer resize the layout and piano for that screen. On your first visit it's auto-detected from your device — you can switch it manually anytime in Settings, and your choice is remembered after that.

Why does the piano scroll sideways instead of showing all the keys?

Keys have a minimum legible width. If squeezing every key (plus the extra octave of context) into a narrow screen would make them too thin to read, the keyboard keeps a readable key size and scrolls horizontally instead.

About this build

What's included

All 12 chromatic keys (C, D♭, D, E♭, E, F, F♯, G, A♭, A, B♭, B), each with Major, Natural Minor, Harmonic Minor, and Melodic Minor — 48 scales total. Every key also has its 5 other diatonic modes (Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian, Mixolydian, Locrian), for 60 more — 108 scales, plus 168 arpeggio patterns and 120 pentatonic/exotic scales. Fingering for the 7 "easy" keys (C, D, G, A, E plus their modes) follows the standard scaffold taught in most method books. The other 7 keys (B, F♯, F, B♭, E♭, A♭, D♭) have irregular fingering that was read directly from Alfred's Basic Piano Library rather than assumed.

Key decisions

  • "Db Minor" plays C♯ minor's notes. True D♭ minor needs 8 flats including a double-flat (B𝄫) and is never actually written or played that way — every musician uses the enharmonic equivalent, C♯ minor, instead. This is a general note about the whole 108-scale system, not about whichever scale you currently have selected.
  • A few mode notes are spelled with a practical enharmonic instead of the "textbook correct" one. Some mode/root combinations respell to whatever a musician would actually write, e.g. F♯ major spells its 7th degree "F" instead of "E♯".
  • Modes reuse each root's own confirmed major-scale fingering. Keeps the physical hand position consistent for a given root across all of its scale types.

What might not be 100% right

Note pitches and scale names are correct throughout. The part that's less certain is exactly which finger number is shown/spoken at a few spots in the irregular keys and their modes, arpeggios, and exotic scales.
